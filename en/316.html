4% of vehicles commercialized in Mexico toward 2025 would be hybrid or electric

Consulting firm JD Power estimates that 9.3 million hybrid and electric vehicles will be sold around the world in 2020. However, the consulting firm expects that for 2030 42% of total car sales worldwide would correspond to hybrid or electric units. In Mexico's case, JD Power estimates that for 2025 there will be 1,705,779 sold units, of which 68,407 will be hybrid or electric, which is to say a 4% of the total sold vehicles.

AMIA proposes measures to counteract the effects of the COVID-19

The Mexican Association of the Automotive Industry (AMIA) proposed to authorities ways in which they might work together and allow fiscal strategies like eliminating, reducing and returning taxes to the industry and its workers, giving credits to employees and medium and small supplier companies, as well as pushing back the entry into force of the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) to July 2021 to be able to meet its requirements.

Isuzu Motors of Mexico announces changes in its directive team

Isuzu Motors of Mexico announced that Koichi Yoda will be returning to Japan and that his position will be filled by Tetsuya Fujita who will become president of the Board and CEO. Another relevant announcement is that Yusuke Ozaki, who worked as subdirector of Sales and Marketing, is finishing his four year and a half cycle in Mexico. The position will be filled by Hiroshi Ikegawa who will also work as Commercial director and who already has an important trajectory in Isuzu Motors of Mexico, from 2009 to 2015.

Ford pushes back the start of operations of its North American plants

Automotive company Ford announced that it will push back the start of operations of its North American plants, including its plants in Mexico, with the objective of protecting its workers from the COVID-19 pandemic. The company had the goal of restarting production on April 6th in its assembly plant in Hermosillo and on April 14 in several key plants in the United States, but now it's pushed the start dates further back, and the new start dates will be announced later.

The Mexican automotive industry asks to be considered essential

Vehicle makers in Mexico are asking to be included in the list of essential sectors that will be able to keep part of their operations during the health emergency declared in the country last Monday March 30th to combat COVID-19 and by which the suspension of non-essential activities was ordered. "We're requesting to both the Ministry of Economy and Health to be considered an essential industry so that we may maintain some sort of operation," said the general director of the Mexican Association of the Automotive Industry (AMIA), Fasto Cuevas to Reuters.

Mexico surpasses China in the export of auto parts to the United States

In the last eight years Chinese auto parts exports to the United States showed zero growth, while sales from Mexico to its northern neighbor increased by 43% informed the president of Mexican Association of Automotive Parts Manufacturers (INA), Oscar Albin. The exports of auto parts from China to the United States went from US$1,756 in value by assembled car in 2012 to a value of US$1,733 by vehicle in 2019. Mexico, on the other hand reported in 2012 auto parts exports with a value of US$3,694 per vehicle and at the close of 2019 the parts value per vehicle increased to US$5,268.

New vehicle sales drop 25.5% in March

During March of 2020, the first month of the health emergency caused by COVID-19, there were a total of 87,517 sold new vehicles in Mexico, which meant an annual drop of 25.5%. Only in a month to month comparison, vehicle sales in the national market registered a decrease of 16.1% from February to March. During March, month in which the health emergency had not yet been declared by authorities, Japanese automaker Nissan managed to sell 17.520 units while General Motors (that manages the Chevrolet, Buick, Cadillac and GMC brands) sold 16,528 vehicles.
