22 May, 2017
Mexico exported US$ 11.55 billion in auto parts to the U.S. during first quarter

During the first quarter 2017, Mexico exported to the U.S. 90% of its auto parts production, which totaled US$ 11.55 billion. That means a 40% of U.S. imports on this segment were supplied by its Southern neighbor, according to Department of Commerce. Mexico remains as top auto parts supplier for the U.S. followed by Canada which provides 12.2% of the components. China and Japan are next with 11.3% and 11.0% shares respectively. Germany, South Korea, United Kingdom and India all together provide less than 20% of the auto parts demanded by the U.S. market.
Trump administration notifies Congress plans to renegotiate NAFTA

The Trump administration formally launched efforts on Thursday to renegotiate NAFTA with Mexico and Canada. By sending a notification letter to US lawmakers saying the administration wants NAFTA to be "modernized", US Trade Representative Robert Lighthizer triggered a 90-day consultation period with Congress and the American public that would allow talks to begin after August 16. In the letter, Lighthizer said that NAFTA needs modernization for digital trade, intellectual property rights, labor and environmental standards, rules for state-owned enterprises, and food safety standards.
Valeo outpaces global auto production by investing on fuel-saving, self-driving tech

France-based automotive supplier Valeo announced its sales increased 22% to outpace global vehicle production in all regions, keeping untouched its 2017 goals on strong demand for its fuel-saving and automated-driving technology. Revenue rose to 4.77 billion euros (US$ 5.19 billion) in January-March from 3.92 billion a year earlier, the company said in a statement. In Mexico, Valeo owns 13 operations where each of its four divisions are involved. The operations include 2 sales-customer service offices, 2 research centers and 9 production sites.
New Faurecia plant on schedule to open by the end of year in Guanajuato

French auto parts manufacturer Faurecia announced it plans to start operations at a new facility in Guanajuato by the end of 2017. The company invested US$ 48 million to set up this plant, located in the municipality of San Jose Iturbide. According to company officials the production site will create around 700 permanent jobs. A ribbon-cutting ceremony is scheduled in the first quarter of 2018. The facility will be Faurecia's 17th plant in the country and the company's fourth dedicated to exhaust systems and emissions control technologies in Mexico.
VW Puebla plant tools up for production of the all-new 2018 Jetta

After seven years manufacturing the Jetta A6 at its Puebla facility, Volkswagen prepares to cease production of the current model to pave the way for the new version of which two units are already built daily to test reliability of the product and make proper adjustments to the assembly line. The German automaker plans to launch the all-new Jetta in early 2018, according to company officials. The company invested US$ 1 billion in tooling for the Jetta and the all-new Tiguan long-wheelbase, which is scheduled to enter showrooms in the last days of July or early August.
Lear chooses Zacatecas to open its 45th plant in Mexico

Lear Corporation opened in 45th plant in Mexico, which required a US$ 21 million investment. The facility is located in the municipality of Calera, Zacatecas. According to company officials, around 1,300 workers were recruited for the first phase of operations to manufactures seat covers. An additional US$ 9.5 million investment is planned to expand operations in 2018, with an opening of 600 more jobs. During the last four years Lear opened 11 plants in the country. The company estimates its Mexican workforce will total 56,000 employees by the end of the year.
Four automakers settle Takata airbag lawsuit for US$ 553.5 million

Four automakers agreed to a US$ 553.5 million settlement to address class-action claims covering owners of nearly 16 million vehicles with potentially defective Takata airbags. Owners of those vehicles are now eligible for financial assistance in getting their vehicles fixed and up to US$ 500 in compensation under the terms. Toyota will cover 9.2 million vehicles by paying US$ 278.5 million, BMW will cover 2.3 million vehicles with a US$ 131 million payment, Subaru plans to cover 2.6 million vehicles by paying US$ 68.2 million and Mazda will write checks for US$ 75.8 million to cover 1.7 million vehicles.
FCA proposes fix to address EPA accusations of emissions cheating

FCA announced it has formally applied for diesel vehicle emissions certification with the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and the California Air Resources Board (CARB) for its 2017 Jeep Grand Cherokee and Ram 1500 diesel vehicles. These vehicles feature updated emissions software calibrations result of months of close collaboration between FCA and EPA and CARB, including extensive testing of the vehicles, to clarify issues related to the Company's emissions control technology. FCA believes the upgrade will address the agencies' concerns.
U.S. regulators approve VW diesel fix for 84,000 vehicles

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency and California Air Resources Board announced approval of a fix for about 84,000 older Volkswagen diesel vehicles that can emit excess emissions. Volkswagen, the world's largest automaker, agreed last year to offer to buy back up to 475,000 2.0-liter diesel vehicles sold in the U.S. or offer fixes if regulators approved. The announcement covers a fix for 84,390 2012-2014 Passat diesel vehicles with automatic transmissions. A fix for vehicles with manual transmissions has not yet been approved. In January, regulators approved a fix for 67,000 2015 model diesels. Around 325,000 older vehicles still await approval for a fix.
U.S. Senate bill to drive research in fuel-saving technologies

Senators from states with major auto plants are trying again to pass legislation designed to encourage investment in zero-emissions Research & Development, and advanced fuel-reduction and safety technologies. The legislation authorizes the Vehicle Technologies Office to be funded at US$ 313.6 million in fiscal year 2018, up from US$ 307 million in 2017, with annual increases of 4% each year through 2022. Sens. Debbie Stabenow, D-Mich., and Lamar Alexander, R-Tenn., co-sponsored the Vehicle Innovation Act, which has the support of automakers and suppliers.
