Mexico's auto production, exports grow at two-digit pace from January through July

Global automakers manufactured last month 286,417 cars and light trucks at facilities in Mexico, 0.7% more than the 284,398 units produced same month of 2016 and a new record high for a month of July. From January through July, a record of 2,170,732 light vehicles rolled out of auto plants in Mexico, an increase of 10.8% compared to the 1,756,390 units produced over the same seven-month period of 2016. It is the first time that automakers reach the 2 million-units milestone in seven months. In July, Mexico exported 243,056 vehicles, which represented a 7.8% increase. Year-to-date exports totaled 1,756,390 light vehicles in the first seven months, up 13.1%.
Gentherm to supply Mercedes-Benz on its first premium segment deal

Michigan-based Gentherm, a developer and manufacturer of thermal management technologies, announced that it has been selected for a next generation Mercedes-Benz car line in the upper or luxury segment. This award represents Gentherm's first advanced seat comfort system program with a major premium luxury German brand. Both companies reserved the rest of the details, including the location of facilities involved in the agreement. However, it is worth noticing that Gentherm operates three production sites in Mexico, two un Acuna, Coahuila, one more in Celaya, Guanajuato. Meanwhile, Mercedes-Benz currently builds a facility in Aguascalientes.
Toyota, Mazda announce joint venture for US plant, electric vehicles and connected cars

Japanese automakers Toyota and Mazda are forming a partnership to build a new final assembly plant in the United States. A location for the US$ 1.6 billion facility has not yet been picked, but it will have the capacity to build 300,000 vehicles annually while employing 4,000 workers, both companies announced. The facility is set to open in 2021. Both firms are also planning to work together to develop electric vehicles and connected-car technologies. A collaboration between the Japanese automakers would not only give them more flexibility and capacity, it would also help them save on the money needed to develop electric vehicles and future technologies.
Instead of sedans, now Toyota plans to build pickups - possibly SUVs - in Guanajuato

Toyota Motor Corp announced it planned to build Tacoma pickups and possibly SUVs at the production plant currently under construction in the state of Guanajuato, Mexico. That facility was originally intended for production of the Corolla sedan, which instead will be built at a new U.S. manufacturing plant the Japanese automaker plans to establish in a joint venture with Mazda. Mazda plans to build SUVs at the same facility. "We're going to concentrate only on pickups at the beginning and are studying the potential for SUVs in the future," Toyota de Mexico spokesman Luis Lozano said. The company's level of investment and jobs is expected to remain similar.
Mazda scores technological breakthrough with compression ignition engine

Mazda has become the world's first automaker to commercialize a compression ignition gasoline engine, meaning it ignites the fuel-air mixture without the need of spark plugs, just through compression, like a diesel engine. This is a technology its rivals have been pursuing for decades with no results so far. The new engine, called Skyactiv-X, is 20 to 30% more fuel efficient than the current models, and its fuel economy potentially matches that of a diesel engine without high emissions of nitrogen oxides or sooty particulates. Mazda already builds about 230,000 Skyactiv engines per year in Guanajuato, but those engines do not feature the new technology yet.
German supplier ZF unveils new column drive steering system for the Jeep Compass

Germany-based auto supplier ZF has launched its first column drive steering system in the US market on the all-new 2017 Jeep Compass, a product which the company claims it brings many advantages of electric steering technologies to U.S. consumers. "The technology can deliver a fuel saving of up to 4% with a corresponding reduction in carbon dioxide emissions when compared to a conventional mechanical rack and pinion steering system," said the company in a statement. This particular system is sold to FCA globally and is manufactured in Mexico for the Jeep Compass, which is built at the FCA Toluca Assembly plant.
UK-based cargo airline CargoLogicAir to open London-to-Mexico City service

UK-based all-cargo airline CargoLogicAir (CLA) announced it will begin twice-weekly London-to-Mexico City service, a route linking the Mexican capital to markets in the United States, via Atlanta, and the carrier's hub in London. The service will start on August 19 with a total cargo capacity of 200 tons a week. It'll be the company's first scheduled cargo route, said Dmitry Grishin, CEO of CargoLogicAir, who added that the new route seeks to attract customers in the automotive industry. CLA will operate 747-400 freighters out of London Stansted Airport every Wednesday and Saturday to Mexico City, via Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport.
Bendix ramps up actuators production at Acuna, Coahuila, facility

Bendix Commercial Vehicle Systems has achieved production milestones across four production facilities to meet increasing market demand, the company reported in a statement. Among those manufacturing sites ramping up production is the Acuna, Coahuila, plant, where the company produces brake actuators for commercial vehicles. This growth is contributing to what is expected to be the highest volume year for actuators since they began production in Bendix's Acuna, Coahuila, manufacturing facility in December 2010. The operation is on pace to produce an average of 8,300 actuators per day, a 10% increase over the 2016 number of 7,600.
Commercial Vehicle Group posts 9% increase in revenue during second quarter

Heavy truck industry supplier Commercial Vehicle Group, Inc. (CVGI) reported second quarter 2017 revenues of US$ 195.1 million compared to US$ 178.3 million in the prior year period, an increase of 9.5%. CVGI highlighted an increase of wire harness production capacity by establishing a new facility in Mexico with better access to labor. "It is taking longer than previously anticipated to manage down the cost associated with the labor shortage - the burden on second quarter results was on the order of US$ 4 million and there may be another US$ 3 million to US$ 6 million of cost in the last half of the year," the company stated in its report.
Intel to build more than 100 autonomous vehicles for testing in US, Israel and Europe

Just hours after its acquisition of auto-visual company Mobileye was completed, Intel announced that it will build a fleet of Level 4, fully self-driving vehicles for testing in the US, Israel, and Europe. The first vehicles will hit the road starting this year, as the fleet eventually scales to more than 100 units. The cars will be Level 4 autonomous, meaning that they will be capable of handling most driving situations themselves, whereas Level 5 is largely theoretical and covers complete automation in any condition. Intel announced plans to acquire Israel-based Mobileye for US$ 15.3 billion back in March, a deal which just closed last Tuesday.
