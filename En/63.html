Takata files for bankruptcy; no jobs cuts or plants closings are expected

Japanese air bag manufacturer Takata Corp. filed for bankruptcy protection today in Tokyo and the United States as it faces ballooning liabilities in connection with an extensive recall of defective inflators linked to the deaths of at least 16 people. Most of Takata's assets will be bought by rival Key Safety Systems for about US$ 1.6 billion. The Chinese-owned company based in suburban Detroit said it won't cut any Takata jobs or close any of Takata's facilities. In Mexico, Takata owns six plants located in the cities of Monclova, Torreon, Acuna, Agua Prieta and two facilities in Monterrey, where it also operates a Technical Center.
Kia celebrates 1 year building cars in Pesqueria, Nuevo Leon

Korean automaker Kia revealed it built 183,290 cars at its Nuevo Leon assembly plant after a full year of operations, which began May 16, 2016. Most of those units, a total 142,739, were exported to several markets, but mostly to the United States. The company expects to build 300,000 Forte, Rio and Hyundai Accent vehicles during 2017 and plans to raise annual output to 400,000 by 2018. Kia will also celebrate on July 1 its second year selling cars in Mexico, a market where it has sold 102,758 vehicles until May 2017, becoming the country's seventh automaker in volume sales at this point.
Mitsubishi might use Nissan Mexican facilities to grow

Mitsubishi will lean heavily on resources from the Renault-Nissan Alliance to grow in the United States and China, Chairman Carlos Ghosn said to Japanese media at a press conference in advance of the automaker's annual shareholders meeting. Ghosn told reporters the company has been unable to expand due to its low resources, but things will change as soon as Mitsubishi starts to explore into the Renault-Nissan "toolbox" of products, technologies and factories. "We are thinking of using the facilities of Renault or Nissan eventually if it makes sense," he said, without referring specifically to Nissan's plants in the United States and Mexico.
All-new VW Tiguan receives mixed reviews from US media during first trial

Last week, Volkswagen revealed the all new Tiguan at a low-key media event driving out of Denver, Colorado, up to 8,000 feet in the Rocky Mountains. Reviews for the compact SUV have been mixed so far, Forbes praised the ergonomics as "top-notch" and questioned if the VW Puebla plant would be able to supply demand. Meanwhile, The Washington Post said the unit was "a solid step in the right direction for VW". On the other side, the specialized website Jalopnik classified the new turbocharged 2.0-liter engine as "sterile". The all-new VW Tiguan arrives this summer to U.S. dealerships at a US$ 25,345 base price plus a US$ 900 destination fee.
Mexico-made Mercedes-Benz cars to have at least 70% NAFTA content

During an interview with El Financiero, Mathias Kolm, Purchasing Manager at Daimler Passenger Cars, said the company has secured at least 70% of NAFTA content for the units Mercedes-Benz plans to build at its Aguascalientes facility starting in 2018. Kolm said Daimler is pleased to add a great number of providers already established in Mexico to its supply chain, even though he offered no figures. "Most of them will have to expand their Mexican operations to meet our demands," he added. The official confirmed the plant jointly built between Infiniti and Mercedes-Benz will have a capacity for 300,000 units a year, half of those made by each company.
US trade representative 'troubled' by Ford's China move

U.S. Trade Representative Robert Lighthizer said he would support taking action against Ford's decision to move some production to China if the shift was because of "non-economic reasons." Lighthizer did not give details on the factors that could trigger action from the administration of President Donald Trump or what sort of action it might take. But he made clear he was not enthused about Ford's announcement earlier this week that it will move some production of its Focus small car to China and import the vehicles to the United States. "I find that very troubling," Lighthizer said. "It doesn't necessarily make sense to me."
Germany-based Prettl Group expands operations in Queretaro

Germany-based manufacturer Prettl Group plans to invest US$ 8 million to build its fourth production site in Queretaro state, fifth in Mexico. Around 200 permanent jobs will be created along with this project which will include corporate offices, but the company also plans to expand output at another site adding 300 more job openings, according to Queretaro state officials. Prettl automotive division operates two plants in Queretaro, located at the Corregidora and Colon municipalities, and one in Comonfort, Guanajuato, where the company manufactures harnesses and electrical components.
New Jersey-based Weiss-Aug opens US$ 15 million plant in Apodaca

New Jersey-based manufacturer Weiss-Aug Co. Inc. opened its first production facility outside the United States in Apodaca, Nuevo Leon, where the company plans to invest US$ 15 million in a three-year period. From a 30,000-square-foot plant the company will provide plastic components for medical equipment, automotive and appliances sectors. For that end Weiss-Aug recruited around 45 workers to develop its first stage of operations, a figure that will triple over the next three years. The company considered Queretaro, Tijuana and Mexicali to establish operations, but it chose Nuevo Leon because of its human capital, Weiss-Aug officials said.
Dodge Grand Caravan output idled at Canadian plant to meet US airbag regulation

Production of the Dodge Grand Caravan for the U.S. market will stop for several months at Fiat Chrysler's Windsor, Ontario, assembly plant, according to a union official who represents workers at the facility where the minivan is built. FCA plans to resume production until December, when output of the 2018 model year Grand Caravan will begin. A former FCA engineer told Automotive News that the current Grand Caravan does not comply with a new federal safety regulation concerning airbags, which is set to go into full effect in September. Production of the Grand Caravan for the Mexican and Canadian markets will not be affected.
GM to cut third shift at Chevy Malibu plant amid slow sales

General Motors in late September will cut the third shift at its Fairfax Assembly Plant in Kansas City, Kansas, where the Chevrolet Malibu midsize sedan amid slowing passenger car sales. The factory is the fifth assembly plant since November that GM has announced would shed shifts. GM has cut the third shifts at car factories including at Lansing Grand River Assembly Plant and the Lordstown Assembly Plant in Ohio, and the second shift at the Detroit-Hamtramck Assembly Plant. GM also cut the third shift at its Lansing Delta Township SUV plant in May. The automaker said 1,000 of the about 3,000 hourly and salaried employees in Fairfax are expected to be affected.
