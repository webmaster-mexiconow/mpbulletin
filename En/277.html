Bulletin #277
October 25th, 2019
Businessmen from the State of Mexico ratify automotive investment of US$31 million

Businessmen from the automotive sector ratified an investment in the State of Mexico of around US$31 million and the generation of more than 1,400 direct jobs for people living in the State of Mexico. This, after the minister of Justice and Human Rights, Rodrigo Espeleta, delivered to members of the Association of Automotive Distributors of the State of Mexico (Amdamex) 23 Unique Feasibility Rulings, which endorse the development of their projects in the entity and which were delivered thanks to the agreement that allows for the exchange of current rulings for permanent ones.
Automotive industry representatives oppose the transitory article in favor of chocolate cars

Representatives from the automotive industry urged the Senate of the Republic to eliminate the fifteenth transitory article written in the 2020 Revenue Law by the deputies, which would endorse the illegality of the vehicles already in the country through laws that would allow vehicle contraband. The president of the Mexican Association of Automotive Distributors (AMDA), Guillermo Prieto, affirmed that the approval of this article opens the doors to the internment of used vehicles coming in from the United States, action for which the Mexican automotive industry was not informed of nor agreed to.
Exports and production for heavy vehicles increases in the first eight months of 2019

The National Association of Producers of Buses, Trucks and Tractor Trucks (ANPACT) informed that 142,315 units were produced during the first eight months of the year, number which, compared to the 113,453 units produced during the same period of 2018 represented an increase of 25.44% As for the accumulated number for exports, these reached a total of 119,014 units, which means an increase of 25.53% when compared to 2018.
The legalization of chocolate cars would not affect premium segment: Alexander Wehr

The approval of a transitory article to legalize imported used cars from the United States, so called 'chocolate' cars, will impact the wholesale automotive market, but not the premium segment, said Alexander Wehr, president and CEO of BMW Group Latin America. The director said in an interview with El Financiero that this is also experienced in other markets of the world like Europe, but they're not affected. "We don't feel affected by this trend, this mostly impacts the basic segment, but I understand that it's an additional impact for the national Mexican market which is now falling at 7.5%," he said.
Nidec invests US$25 million in new plant

Last week Motores de Reynosa Nidec laid the first stone to begin construction of its second plant in charge of engine manufacturing for the robotic industry. Nidec announced that it will have a surface superior to 150,000 square meters, with the possibility of an additional 50,000 square meter expansion and will be built with an investment of US$25 million and will generate around 400 direct jobs. The new plant is located in the Villa Florida Industrial Park.
Toyota asks Senate to stop the legalization of chocolate cars

Before the approval in the Chamber of Deputies of an initiative in the 2020 Revenue Law that seeks to legalize used imported cars from the United States, also known as 'chocolate' cars Luis Lozano, government relations director for Toyota Mexico, said that he hopes senators will halt this project. "We're calling for the consideration of all political groups so that they may reconsider the subject, and since all of this has to pass through the Senate, we hope there's voices that can support us," said the director.
Visteon inaugurates design and engineering center with investment of over US$9 million

Visteon, company dedicated to the manufacturing of components for automatic and electric vehicle cabins, inaugurated in Queretaro a design and engineering center in which it employs 600 people, with an investment of a little over US$9 million. The American company is a Tier1 supplier, specialized in electronic cabins, and started operations in the state in 2017, with a total of 160 people as part of its staff in the company's production center.
Faurecia invests US$12 million in San Luis Potosi plant expansion

With an investment of US$12 million, the company Faurecia expanded its "Frames" plant in San Luis Potosi, from 16,000 to 35,000 square meters, with the objective of strengthening their operations and increasing their productive capacity, adding to their workforce 200 new direct jobs. The plant's manager, Javier de la Cruz, said that there will be new productive areas which will have cutting-edge technology in Industry 4.0, Digital Enterprise, Automatic Guided Vehicles (AGV) y Radio Frequency Identification (RFID), which will favor them in bettering their quality and production standards.
Sarrel plant in Guanajuato is inaugurated

The French Tier 2 auto parts company invested US$30 million in its 12,000 square meter plant, thus generating 250 jobs. The plant includes a chrome plating line and an injection line for pieces like speedometer, handles, logos, etc. With this plant, Sarrelmex plans to reach annual sales of US$20 million and highlights its position for the North American market like one of the top companies in chrome plating and injection. They also have a plant in Portugal and their main clients are Autoliv, Plastic Ómnium, Faurecia, Valeo and BHTC.
Congress will review the chocolate car legalization initiative

The Senate committed itself to reviewing the Morena initiative that hopes to legalize chocolate cars, which was included in the 2020 Revenue Law. Ricardo Monreal, president of Congress's Political Coordination Board, said that they will act like a genuine reviewing Chamber. To that effect, the national automotive industry trusts that the legalization of chocolate cars will be stopped by the Senate, which is why they haven't prepared a plan B, said Eduardo Solis, president of the Mexican Association of the Automotive Industry (AMIA).
