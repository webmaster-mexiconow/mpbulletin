Goodyear's new plant in San Luis Potosi starts trial production

Goodyear's new plant in San Luis Potosi is 100% completed with trial production under way, it's just a matter of weeks for the company to officially start operations, reported local media. The 1 million-plus-square-feet facility, located in the WTC II industrial park, is Goodyear's first new factory in the Americas in 25 years. The plant represents a US$ 550 million investment and is scheduled to manufacture six million tires a year, including the Goodyear Eagle F1 and the Goodyear Wrangler All-Terrain Adventure with Kevlar. The company broke ground on the project in July 2015.
Here's top three automakers in Mexico by production, exports

Mexico's top three automakers by production output from January through August are Nissan with 582,047 units, followed by General Motors with 495,660 cars and pickups, while FCA ended up in third place to 411,230 vehicles, according to figures by the Mexican Automotive Industry Association (AMIA). On an exports basis though, General Motors topped the ranking to 409,976 vehicles, followed by FCA with 385,436 units and Nissan was displaced to third position to 320,148 cars and pickups.
Ford confirms next-gen Fiesta won't be offered in the US market

Ford Performance of Europe Director, Leo Roeks, confirmed to CarBuzz at the Frankfurt International Auto Show there will be no next-generation Ford Fiesta in the U.S. market. "It's simply a matter of a lack of interest in the B-segment in America. It doesn't make sense, nor is it possible financially," Roeks said. While the future for the Mexico-made Fiesta has been decided, it's still unclear what will happen to the Cuautitlan Assembly Plant where the compact car is built. According to Just-auto.com, a C-segment crossover will debut in 2019 and it's likely to be built in Mexico, but Ford has yet to decide whether it will roll out of Cuautitlan or the Hermosillo plant.
FCA to shift Dodge Journey production from Mexico to Italy

Automotive News reports that a new Dodge Journey will arrive for 2019 and it'll be based on Alfa Romeo's new rear-drive Giorgio platform. For that end, production of the Journey successor will move from Toluca, Mexico to Italy, according to the news outlet. Last month, the Dodge Journey sales sank 52% to 5,665 units, but year-to-date sales are still up 5% to 69,342 crossovers. "While the Chrysler brand will fill the midsize, three-row crossover segment for FCA in 2019, the Journey will live on for Dodge as a rear-wheel-drive, two-row performance crossover on the Giorgio platform, which it will share with the Alfa Romeo Stelvio," says the report.
Volvo US plant to be twice as big as previously announced

Volvo Cars is doubling investment in its first U.S. plant to add a second production line at a total cost of US$ 1 billion, revealed in recent days the Post and Courier newspaper, a media outlet based in Charleston, South Carolina, where the facility is currently under construction. The move to raise capacity even before completion of the plant will ultimately add 2,500 workers to the 2,000 already being recruited by the Swedish carmaker ahead of its 2018 opening, a source told Canadian news agency Reuters. Volvo's marketing officials revealed in recent days the plant will rely on deliveries from at least 29 suppliers operating in Mexico.
Canadian metal stampings manufacturer builds its fourth plant in Mexico

Matcor Matsu, a Canadian manufacturer of metal stampings for body-in-white, interior and seating applications, broke ground last week at the Amistad Aeropuerto Industrial park in Ramos Arizpe, Coahuila, to build a 247,000 square feet plant. According to local media, the company plans to invest US$ 90 million and create 550 permanent jobs to build seating and panel structures for General Motors and Fiat Chrysler Automobiles. The Ramos Arizpe facility will be Matcor Matsu's fourth operation in Mexico, since it already operates two manufacturing facilities of 126,000 and 69,000 square feet in Saltillo and a 90,000 square feet plant through a joint venture.
Mecaplast-Key Plastics rebrands itself as Novares Group

Mecaplast-Key Plastics, a company formed after French group Mecaplast acquired Michigan-based Key Plastics LLC, has unveiled its new brand identity to be ‘Novares Group SA.' The Paris-based firm manufactures automotive interior, exterior and under-the-hood plastic components. Novares has seven automotive product lines, including engine components, electric powertrain components for hybrid and electric vehicles, exterior paint and surfaces, interior and exterior handles, air vents and decorative trims, bezels and clusters, and interior and car body trim. In Mexico, the company operates manufacturing sites in Chihuahua, Silao and Puebla.
Prices for the 2018 Nissan Versa Note to remain unchanged for the US market

Nissan announced that U.S. pricing for the 2018 Nissan Versa Note hatchback, which is on sale now at Nissan dealers nationwide, will remain unchanged from 2017 prices. Additionally, for 2018 the sporty Versa Note SR grade adds standard Intelligent Key with Push Button Ignition, Nissan Vehicle Immobilizer System and Easy Fill Tire Alert. Manufacturer's Suggested Retail Prices (MSRP) for the 2018 Nissan Versa Note models are US$ 15,480 for the Versa Note 1.6 S Xtronic, US$ 16,380 for the Versa Note 1.6 SV Xtronic and US$ 17,980 for the Versa Note 1.6 SR Xtronic. Both Versas sedan and hatchback for the North American market are manufactured in Aguascalientes.
WABCO takes full ownership of joint venture with Meritor

WABCO Holdings Inc., a global supplier of technologies and services to improve safety, efficiency and connectivity of commercial vehicles, announced that it will further expand its commitment and operations in the commercial vehicle market in North America by taking full ownership of the Meritor WABCO joint venture. WABCO has signed an agreement to purchase Meritor, Inc.'s stake in the joint venture for US$ 250 million. WABCO operates through its subsidiaries two facilities located in San Luis Potosi and Empalme, Sonora. Meritor owns two production facilities in Nuevo Leon, one of those also serves as distribution and engineering center.
White House uses OECD data to push for tougher NAFTA origin rules

Seeking to make its case for tougher rules of origin in NAFTA, the Trump administration released a new study that shows U.S. value-added content is declining for manufactured goods imported from Mexico and Canada. The Commerce Department analysis of recently released OECD trade in value-added data found that U.S. content in automotive imports from Mexico declined to 18.1% in 2011 from 26.5% in 1995. U.S. content in automotive imports from Canada declined to 26.4% from 34.9% over the same period. The report comes as the third round of NAFTA talks started in Ottawa, where the rules of origin are about to be discussed.
