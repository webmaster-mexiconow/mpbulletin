The Mexican automotive industry expects a 30% fall in the production and sale of vehicles

The Mexican automotive industry is facing the "worst crisis" in its history and it is projecting "critical" scenarios toward the close of the year, with falls of 30% on average in the production of light vehicles, auto parts, internal and external sales. Fausto Cuevas, director of the Mexican Association of the Automotive Industry (AMIA), estimated that the production of light vehicles will contract by 30% and exports by 33%. Oscar Albin, president of the Mexican Association of Automotive Parts Manufacturers (INA) said that they expect a drop of 32% in auto parts and components turnover. On the other hand, vehicle sales in distributors will have an impact of 30% by the close of 2020, said Guillermo Rosales, director of the Mexican Association of Automotive Distributors (AMDA).

Car exports to the United States fall 94.5% in May

Mexico turned out to be the most affected light vehicle provider in the American market during May, due to the automotive sector's inactivity caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, by dropping 95.5%, which is contrasted against its competitors which also presented falls, but in percentages of less than 50%, reported the Mexican Association of the Automotive Industry (AMIA). According to AMIA's director, Fausto Cuevas, the participation of Mexican cars in the American market placed at 1.2% during May, when they used to place at s monthly average of 16%.

General Motors of Mexico implements "Total Sanitization" program

General Motors of Mexico has started its "Total Sanitization" program, which contemplates integral measures to care for the health of their clients, collaborators and suppliers in its distributor network throughout the country as they gradually restart operations. The initiative has the goal of preparing vehicles, administrative areas, sales floors and service centers to avoid COVID-19 contagions. The "Total Sanitization" program is expected to be implemented in distributors in the Chevrolet, Buick, GMC and Cadillac brands in the country before, during and after the process of visiting sales floors or service at home, as the case may be.

Heavy vehicle manufacturers will meet with the new USMCA requirements

Manufacturing companies for heavy vehicles are considering meeting the new requirements in the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) from the start. Miguel Elizalde, president of the National Association of Producers of Buses, Trucks and Tractors (ANPACT), explained that-at this time-no companies have notified him of their interest of adhering to the alternative staging regime, which allows for the uniform regulation of the USMCA. Although he did say that in the case of heavy vehicles, the origin rule is modified in four years, so they will keep the regional content at 60% until mid-2024.

There's a need for control in the import of heavy vehicles: ANPACT

Before the impact caused by the COVID-19 pandemic on the heavy vehicle industries in Mexico and the arrival of used units, there's a need for strict control in customs and boost the internal market, indicated Miguel Elizalde, executive president of the National Association of Producers of Buses, Trucks and Tractors (ANPACT). Elizalde commented that there's an urgent need for a strict control in the import of these types of units, since "a lot of these vehicles are imported through fiscal fraud, which is to say, they are undervalued or don't meet requirements. We're in favor of free trade, but not on the imports of scrap vehicles."

Automotive sector jobs are at risk

"We're starting back up, but we don't really know what's coming moving forward. Component demand depends on vehicle sales, but these have fallen," said Ulrick Thima, president of the Automotive Cluster of the Central Zone (Clauz), who doesn't rule out that there might be turn reductions, firings or even some plants closing down on the year's second semester. Although auto parts plants are now facing the biggest challenges to keep the 80,000 jobs generated on a national level, assembly plants might not be able to escape personnel cuts.

Practically all light vehicle assemblers will ask for incorporation into the alternative staging regime: AMIA

Before the entry into force of the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) on July 1st it's estimated for assembly plants to need more time to meet the new rules, particularly those regarding regional content. However, companies that are not able to meet with the newly established rules of origin will be able to adhere to the Alternative Staging Regime. On this, AMIA's president, Fausto Cuevas, assured that it's very likely that all light vehicle assemblers in Mexico will be requesting their incorporation into this Alternative Staging Regime.

The automotive industry asks for the reopening of sales floors

Most new automobile sales floors in the country are closed at the moment, since they're not considered an essential activity, which is why the automotive industry has faced challenges in selling their vehicles for two months now. That is why, AMDA, the Mexican Association of the Automotive Industry (AMIA), the National Association of Producers of Buses, Trucks and Tractors (ANPACT) and the Mexican Association of Automotive Parts Manufacturers (INA) have requested the federal government to allow automotive industry sales floors to reopen on the premise that the sector has the necessary protocols to safe-keep employees' and visitors' health.
