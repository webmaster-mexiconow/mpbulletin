Bulletin #15
5 January, 2017
Truck plants in North America to end year running overtime

Record sales paces in the U.S., Canada and Mexico have kept plants busy. About 15 North American plants reportedly were running overtime through last week, nearly four times as many as the same period a year ago, according to the Automotive News Data Center. Most of those plants build trucks.
North American production forecast drops to 17.6 million units for 2017

IHS Markit forecasts North American production will drop from 17.85 million units this year to 17.6 million in 2017. Analysts say the key in 2017 will be how automakers continue to handle market demands for cars vs. light trucks. Car plants will likely struggle while light-truck plants will continue to run at high levels.
Nissan's global production increased 17% during November

Nissan's global production in November increased 17.0 percent year-on-year to 539,678 units, the tenth consecutive month of increase and a record for a single month, according to a press release. In the U.S., production increased 0.3% to 82,335 units, a record for the month of November. In Mexico, production increased 5.3% to 74,122 units, also a record.
Hyundai Motor slashes executives' perks to reduce costs

Headed for a fourth straight annual profit decline, Hyundai Motor is trimming its cost by scaling back on business class flights and annual family home trips for overseas employees, executives told Reuters. The South Korean automaker has been hit by its exposure to weak emerging markets, and a product line-up that features more sedans than SUVs, just as the latter have become more popular across many markets.
A 35% tariff on imported vehicles would cost jobs, auto sales in U.S.

Data from the nonprofit Center for Automotive Research (CAR) suggests a 35 percent tariff on imported vehicles would lead to the loss of 6,700 auto industry jobs in the United States due to higher costs. The tariff would also reduce the number of total vehicles sold annually in the United States by 450,000, according to the CAR, which is funded by auto manufacturers.
Ford Fusions under investigation for potential brake problem

U.S. safety regulators are investigating Ford Motor Co.'s Fusion and Mercury Milan sedans from the 2007 to 2009 model years for brakes that may fail in certain conditions. There have been three reported crashes related to this issue, but no injuries, the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration and Ford officials said.
Visteon to debut its next-generation infotainment platform at CES 2017

Visteon Corporation will showcase its latest innovations for the connected car and beyond at Consumer Electronics Show 2017 January 5-8 in Las Vegas. Headlining Visteon's exhibit will be the debut of its next-generation infotainment platform, Phoenix, which is designed to unlock innovation by enabling third-party developers to create apps easily. It will also feature an in-vehicle augmented reality windshield head-up display (HUD) that overlays virtual information directly in the driver's sight.
Toyota announces executive changes in North America

Toyota Motor North America announced executive changes effective as of January 3, 2017. The changes announced include David Fernandes as president of Toyota Motor Manufacturing Alabama, Millie Marshall as president of Toyota Motor Manufacturing Indiana, Leah Curry as president of Toyota Motor Manufacturing West Virginia and Akitoshi Ichino as president of Toyota Motor Manufacturing Mississippi. Each will have responsibilities for all manufacturing and administrative operations at the plants located in Huntsville, Alabama; Princeton, Indiana; Buffalo, West Virginia and Tupelo, Mississippi, respectively.
LS Automotive to make public offering to build plants in Mexico, U.S.

LS Automotive Corp., an automotive electronic parts making unit of South Korea-based LS Group, will go public in the first half of next year by offering 30 percent of its shares with the aim of raising a maximum of US$ 82.9 million. The proceeds will be used to set up new production facilities in overseas countries including the United States, Mexico, China and India, the company announced.
Uber moves self-driving cars testing from California to Arizona

Uber Technologies removed its self-driving test cars from California after the state's Department of Motor Vehicles revoked the registration of 16 vehicles, which the regulator said lacked the proper permits. "Our cars departed for Arizona this morning by truck," an Uber spokeswoman said in a written statement. "We'll be expanding our self-driving pilot there in the next few weeks."
